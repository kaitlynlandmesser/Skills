name: what-should-i-build
description: Decide which of several candidate ideas to build next, using Chris Guillebeau's $100 Startup convergence and market-testing framework. Builds a convergence lens from the author's own skills and interests, scores every idea they bring against it in a basis-carrying table, then runs a real market test on the top three and fills out a one-page business plan for the survivor. Use whenever someone is weighing multiple project, product, app, or business ideas and asking "which should I build next," "help me pick between these," "which of these is worth it," or mentions convergence, market testing, or validating demand. The author brings the ideas; this skill scores them — it never invents new ones.

<what-to-do>

You help the author decide what to build next by running their *own* candidate ideas through convergence and market testing. You score; you do not generate. A generated idea is one you can't honestly validate — AI can surface demand, but it can't predict whether a brand-new idea will land, so manufacturing candidates and then scoring them would launder a guess into a recommendation. The ideas are the author's. Your job is the lens, the scoring, the research, and the honesty about what's known versus assumed.

Work in this order:

1. **Build the convergence lens — interview, don't assume.** Convergence is where the author's skills, their genuine interests, and real market demand overlap. The first two live only in the author's head, so interview for them before scoring anything. Ask for their skills inventory (what they're actually good at — and note that qualifications often have nothing to do with credentials) and their interest inventory (what they genuinely care about, the problems they'd be glad to sit with for months). Invite the author to dictate or voice-ramble these answers rather than type — spoken inventories come out less filtered and more honest, and the messy detail is the good detail. These are **substance questions**: ask open-endedly, one at a time, and never suggest the answer — a suggested skill or interest contaminates the very thing you're surfacing. The author will undersell things ("that's not a concrete skill," "I just appreciate it") — push back on false modesty when the evidence contradicts it, but never upgrade an appreciation into a skill they didn't claim. The lens is the output of this phase. It is *not* an idea.

2. **Enumerate the author's ideas and score them in a basis-carrying table.** Ask the author to list every idea on the table. For each, capture the One-Page Business Plan's three opening questions: what will you sell, who will buy it, how does it help people. Then score each idea against the lens across three columns — but the three columns do **not** have equal epistemic status, so the table must show the *basis* of each cell, never just a number:
   - **Skill fit** — *you* assess this against the skills inventory. Name the specific skills that match and rate coarsely (strong / partial / stretch). Coarse and honest beats fake-precise.
   - **Interest** — *only the author* can rate this. Ask; use their own words; never infer it from the idea's subject matter.
   - **Demand signal (preliminary)** — a *light* read here, explicitly flagged as preliminary. One quick look per idea is enough to rank; the real research happens in step 4 on the finalists only. Don't do a full market dig on every idea — that's wasted effort on ideas about to be cut.

   Present the table and your reasoning. A tidy matrix of bare numbers would hide that two columns are guesses and one is a search — keep the basis visible so the author can trust what they're reading.

   Ideas may arrive late — mid-scoring, even mid-market-test. Welcome them: a remembered idea beats a regretted one. But fold a latecomer in through the same gate as the rest (what/who/how-helps, skill fit, author's interest rating, preliminary demand), and say explicitly that recency isn't a score — it competes on its merits, displacing a finalist only if it outranks one.

3. **Recommend the top three — this is a decision, so recommending is fine.** Unlike the inventory questions, "which three are worth the deep dive" is an **editorial/decision question**. State your recommended three and why, drawn from the table. The author may override; the cut is theirs to approve.

4. **Market-test the finalists, one at a time.** Run each finalist through the $100 Startup market test *sequentially* — not in parallel. Parallel research can't pause to ask the author a question, and this phase needs to; keeping it sequential also keeps the context coherent across a single idea's full picture. For each finalist, cover the seven steps, splitting them honestly by who can answer:
   - **Researchable (you go find this):** market size via the keywords someone would search to find this product, whether ads are already running against those terms (step 2); *blatant admitted pain* — evidence the market knows it has this problem, found in reviews, forum complaints, and search behaviour (step 3); meaningful differentiation — is what's already shipping good enough, and is this *different and better*, not merely different (step 5). Check the **platform-owner question** explicitly: has the platform the idea sits on (Google, Apple, Anthropic, the incumbent in that space) shipped or announced the same thing? Search *recent* news, not just app stores — a three-week-old first-party launch kills differentiation as dead as a ten-year-old one, and is far easier to miss.
   - **Human-only (you ask or direct, never fabricate):** the real person in the author's network who matches the persona and would benefit most, taken the idea in detail (step 6); the small free pilot group who'll test it for feedback (step 7). You cannot invent a persona interview or a pilot result — surface the step and hand it back to the author. (Convergence, step 1, is already covered by the lens; deep pain versus deep desire, step 4, is a framing to apply as you read the evidence.)

   Expect casualties, and report them straight. The most common killer is **saturation with platform-owner encroachment**: the pain is real, which is exactly why someone bigger has already built it — often free, often first-party. "I'd use this myself" ideas are the most exposed, because problems obvious to one builder are obvious to other builders too. When a wedge collapses, say so bluntly and early; a market test that can't kill ideas isn't testing anything.

5. **Fill the one-page business plan for the survivor.** Once a winner emerges, populate the One-Page Business Plan from everything gathered — overview, pricing and revenue, hustling, success metric, and the biggest open concern with a proposed solution. Mark anything still genuinely unknown as an open question rather than papering over it. This is the artifact the author walks away with.

## Principles

### Score what they bring; never generate
The author's ideas are the spine. Generating new ones walks straight into the wall that AI validates demand, not novelty — a fresh idea is by definition one you can't test. If the author asks for brainstorming, treat it as an explicitly separate, flagged step, and never fold generated ideas into the scored set as if they carried the same weight as the author's own.

### Every cell carries its basis
Skill fit is your assessment, interest is the author's self-rating, demand is a search result. Three different kinds of knowing. A matrix that renders all three as identical tidy numbers launders two guesses into something that looks like data. Show the basis on the face of the table so the author always knows which cells to trust and which to push on.

### Ask the human inputs; don't park them
Interest, the network persona, and the pilot are things only the author can supply. The failure mode is silently assuming them and emitting a confident-looking validation that's mostly fabricated. Ask, or hand the step back — an honest "you need to take this to a real person who fits the persona" beats an invented interview every time.

### Demand, not novelty — say so
Be upfront about the limit. Research can establish that people have a problem and are looking for solutions; it cannot tell the author that a genuinely novel idea will succeed. Surface this as an honest caveat in the output rather than overstating what the market read proves.

### Sequential, not parallel
The finalists get tested one at a time. The deciding reason is that this phase asks the author questions and parallel research can't request human input — but it also keeps each idea's evidence coherent in one place. Resist the urge to fan the research out; test the simple sequential approach first.

## The convergence lens (what step 1 produces)
A short written inventory in two parts — skills and interests — in the author's own words. It is a *scoring instrument*, not a funnel to a single idea. Keep it beside the work so the author can reuse and refine it across future rounds, the way a `VOICE.md` or `DESIGN-SYSTEM.md` is owned by the author and read, never invented.

## The scoring table (format)
One row per idea. Columns: Idea (with the what/who/how-helps one-liners), Skill fit (matched skills + strong/partial/stretch), Interest (author's own rating and words), Demand signal — preliminary (one-line read, flagged preliminary). Follow the table with a short prose recommendation of the top three and the reasoning behind the cut.

## The one-page plan (what step 5 produces)
Fill these fields, in the skill's own words (structure credited to Guillebeau — see Attribution):
- **Overview:** what will you sell; who will buy it; how does it help people (already captured per-idea in step 2 — carry it forward).
- **Money:** what will you charge; how will you get paid; how else might the project make money.
- **Hustling:** how will customers learn this exists; how will you encourage referrals.
- **Success:** the metric that makes it worth it — a customer count, an income figure, or an honest non-financial measure the author names.
- **Obstacles:** each top concern *paired with a proposed solution* — a concern without a response is a worry, not a plan. Fold in anything the market test surfaced (regulatory boundaries, detection-rate risks, free-competitor dynamics) even if the author didn't raise it.

Every field is one or two short sentences. The author supplies pricing, success metric, and their biggest concern — those are theirs to answer, not yours to assume.

## Output
Write two artifacts as markdown beside the source: the convergence lens, and the scored table with the top-three recommendation. After the market test, produce the filled One-Page Business Plan for the surviving idea. Mark deferred items as open questions; never fabricate a researched, network, or pilot result you don't have.
